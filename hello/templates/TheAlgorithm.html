Apples and Oranges.

How do you compare China’s 2.7 Million troops, Uruguay’s 68,037 sq. miles, Equatorial Guinea’s $38,699 GDP Per Capita, the 1,700 people who immigrated to the US in 2014 from Bhutan, Germany’s Score of 15 on the Ease of Doing Business rank, and Mexico’s HDI of 0.76? All variables surveyed by QuantPolitik are aspects of power or importance in the international arena, but their relevance varies across categories, as a $3 trillion GDP is a much greater indicator of “importance” than a country’s size. However, a country’s size matters nonetheless, as evidenced by the Second Congo War, which spilled over to numerous neighboring countries, something less likely to happen in a small island nation. Since all these variables are “apples and oranges” and across all categories the scales measure wildly different things, while the scales additionally vary in their beginnings/ends and increments. Consequently, a way is needed to normalize the variables across all categories, in addition to fitting all scales to same size. The solution is determining the percentile rank of each country for each variable, thus attaining a standard score across all variables (0-100), and a standard scale with which to measure.

The Who in a QP Score.

International Relations, or simply IR, are the study and analysis of relationships between States. These bilateral (country-to-country) relationships are not particularly dissimilar from the interpersonal relationships that two individuals may experience. Wherein, family (same language), marriage (alliances), friendship (governmental leadership interaction), clubs (IMF/G20), work associates (trade flows), and shared neighborhoods (OAS). If there is someone who you’re not friends with, related to, romantically involved, live near, work with, or even associate with, it would be hard to call that person a friend, or someone with whom you’re generally concerned about.

Bilateral relations are the same way; if two countries are far away from one another, and they don’t trade together, their leaders don’t meet with one another, they both have sharply different views on contentious issues like democracy and human rights, and one is especially wealthy and big, while the other is poor and small, would you expect them to be best friends? Of course not! Which is why Eritrea, a tiny, extremely poor country, with a horrendous human rights record and limited strategic value preforms miserably on the QuantPolitik ranking. While a country like the United Kingdom; a nation whose language we borrowed, trade with prolifically, constantly interact with at the highest echelons of government, and who posses tremendous instruments of power (nukes, UNSC seat), in addition to a government that respects liberty/human rights and allows visa free access to American citizens, would be expected to be considered a best friend.

The Where in a QP Score?

QuantPolitik approaches international bilateral relations in the same way any person would approach the question of who are my friends? We first break down the question of what is friendship on the international level? And then we evaluate how each country performs in each area.

At the end of the day, the way we judge who are friends are, time spent together/shared interests/shared history/shared activities/similar outlooks/similar lifestyles, is not especially different from the way QuantPolitik judges and evaluates international bilateral relations.

QuantPolitik sees IR as being composed of 9 core areas, while each core area, composed of about 3-5 variables each:

  2. Business Relations

Why Have a QP Score.

QuantPolitik is the first attempt to quantify international relations. Since the birth of the nation state diplomacy has always been characterized in qualitative terms: “Jim Mattis Says U.S. Is ‘Shoulder to Shoulder’ With Japan”, “Bush Tries to Mend Relations With Mexico”, “Better Relations With Cuba”, “New Tensions With Iran”. All are NY Times headlines from the past few years and all describe bilateral relationships in qualitative terms. But imagine we spoke about the economy or science in the same way? “Financiers believe the Dow has risen today”, “Scientists take darkening mood as sign climate change is accelerating.” Why can’t international relations escape qualitative descriptions and be subject to precise measurement in the same way virtually everything else in modern world is? Well this is the fist attempt to do so.

Predict vs. Reflect

While a regression analysis is usually the best method to asses distinct data and to draw conclusions from it, in this case, however, it would be inappropriate, namely because regressions predict; yet bilateral relations are inherently unpredictable. Consequently, QuantPolitik seeks only to reflect the current state of bilateral relations, and have therefore determined that a standardized score is the best way to do so.

A simple example on the unpredictability of bilateral relations:

During the last week of March 2017, many top officials in the US government had commented on Bashar al-Assad and that he was unlikely to go. Meanwhile, Trump had heaped praise on Russia, and Russia had returned the favor. Anyone who sought to predict the future of US-Syrian relations or US-Russian relations would have predicted that ties were destined to grow closer. However, on April 4th, Assad launched chemical weapons against Khan Shaykhun, and three days later Trump ordered 59 missiles launched against the Shayrat airfield in Syria, leading to strained ties with Russia and the end of each side’s overly gushing rhetoric. US officials also shifted from discussing a future with Assad to stating that he had to go.

Assad’s decision to use chemical weapons against his own people could not be predicted, no matter how fancy the math, nor could the responses of any government be predicted either. However, the missiles launched could be reflected in data and the absence of a Putin-Trump summit can also be captured in the nonappearance of any meetings between the two. While, the supposed rapprochement between the US-Russia at the expense of China (as was assumed in January 2017), will not be reflected, rather the rapprochement between the US-China at the expense of Russia will be.

QuantPolitik does not seek to engage in parlor games of how bilateral relations will unfurl, rather we seek to assess what trends-as reflected in the data-have occurred and what are the consequences.

Algorithm vs. Formula

Since this project seeks to express qualitative information quantitatively, it could readily be described as a formula. An algorithm differs from a formula in that an algorithm describes a set of instructions to be followed. Both may take you to the same place, the answer, yet one (a formula) implies a high correlation with fact or assurance. Whereas an algorithm has more of a lexical “best approach” or “best yet” kind of a feel. The formula implies a solution that works while the algorithm implies it's the best we got so far. I am therefore acknowledging that the terminology of a formula, while more definitionally applicable, has unearned connotations that ultimately would subtract from this project which is more a recipe; throw x-related data together, brew/analyze daily, pour/quantify as a QP Score.

Addendum

I’d also like to add that by no means is this score perfect. Nor is all the data included as up-to-date, nor reflective as any data scientist would like. In addition, some will certainly see this entire project as an exercise in futility, since International Relations is “inherently unquantifiable.” These and other concerns have most assuredly been at the forefront of consideration during the construction of this platform. I’ll be the first to admit that there are significant areas for improvement. However, this is only the first version of the first attempt by anyone to quantify bilateral relations, and that any and all critiques are more than welcome. Any oversights, I’ll be happy to correct, and any additions I’ll be happy to make room for. As this is a continuing project, currently undertaken by just myself, what is displayed on this site is solely, the best attempt thus far to quantify bilateral relations.
Practice makes perfect.
