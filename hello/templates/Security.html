Security

In this domain, America’s Security relationships are evaluated, taking into account, which countries host US Military forces, whether or not they enhance or subtract from their Security environment, their potential for aggressive confrontation and shared multilateral organization. This domain seeks to evaluate whether or not the selected country is powerful or not and if they are receptive to US military Interests. High scorers are powerful and capable nations that are receptive to US interests, while low scorers are weak countries that are less receptive to US forces.

Military Power

Military power measures two key variables, how many of country A’s troops are stationed in Country B, and how many bases Country A has in Country B. The relevance is obvious. The more troops stationed in that country, the closer the two countries must be in order to sustain such a level of support. While both countries will also have a continuing desire to maintain close ties as-military personnel are stationed there. Additionally, the stationing of any deployment is evidence of close ties in the first place and is an important measurement. The second aspect of the number of bases measures the permeating influence of Country A, whether their presence is isolated to one base, or widely dispersed throughout the countryside, therefore a country with a high score in this field, hosts a significant number of military personnel over a dispersed area-thus necessitating very close ties between both states.

Foreign Power

This sub-domain evaluates how much each country spends on their military and how many standing personnel they have in their armed forces. The total dollars spent per annum on defense can be a useful metric for evaluating raw power capabilities, as the more a country spends on defense the more they can afford to do. Obviously just because a country spends significant sums on defense does not necessarily mean that their forces are second-to-none, but it does mean that at a minimum their kit will be up to snuff. In this vein, the number of standing personnel in their armed forces is a useful metric of raw power capabilities, but not of power projection (they still need the ability to transport their forces), or of military efficiency/capability. However, it can be stated that the more troops one has at their disposal the more things they can do, and the more a country can do, the greater the imperative for closer relations.

Nuclear Technology

This sub-domain evaluates whether or not a country has nuclear weapons, and if so whether or not that country’s nuclear program is benign, yet capable of striking Country A (in which case Country B receives 4 points), whether or not Country B hosts Country A’s nuclear weapons (in which case Country B receives 3.5 points-usually through NATO), whether Country B’s nuclear program is benign-yet lacks the capability of striking Country A (in which case Country B receives 3 points). If Country B has no nuclear program it receives 2 points, and if their nuclear program is viewed as antagonistic to Country A, it receives 0 points.

Alliance Climate

In this area the number of alliances, intergovernmental and multilateral organizations shared by both Country A and Country B are evaluated. The sampled multilateral organizations are: NATO, the WTO, the IMF, OAS, G20, G8/7, OECD, ECHELON, and APEC; in later versions of this project a more exhaustive list will be formulated. While Alliances are coded according to whether either country has a multual defense pact with the other, or a collectiv secuity arrangement. If a country also has an intelligence alliance (Five Eyes), they receive an additional 5 points.

Composite Rankings

This sub-domain incorporates the “Composite Index of National Capability” which tabulates each country’s capability to prosecute hostilities, based on factors such as size, population, industrial capacity and military strength. The Global Peace index comes from the Institute for Economics and Peace and measures a country’s proclivity for violence and its commitment to peace.
